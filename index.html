What happened

The US-23 Improvement Study revised its northern boundary to just north of Earhart Road before noise analysis was conducted. Chapel Hill and neighboring communities, originally within the study area, were removed without notification to residents. This change was documented in MDOT technical materials published November 2025 but never communicated directly to affected homeowners.

Background

The Problem: A Gap Between Two Studies

Two active MDOT studies address highway noise in Ann Arbor. Neither covers the northeast corridor where thousands of households live alongside M-14 and US-23.

Study Coverage Area Chapel Hill Included?
M-14 Noise Analysis (PA 121 of 2024) Newport Road to North Maple Road No
US-23 Improvement Study I-94 to Earhart Road (revised Nov. 2025) No — removed before noise analysis ran
Gap Corridor (our neighborhoods) M-14 from Pontiac Trail overpass east to US-23; US-23 south to Plymouth Road Directly affected. Never studied.

The US-23 Improvement Study approved six sound barriers within its corridor following a resident vote in summer 2025. Communities just north of the revised boundary received no equivalent analysis and no opportunity to vote. The dual-study gap is the structural explanation for why this corridor remains unaddressed.

How this was discovered: On March 23, 2026, a conference call convened with State Senator Sue Shink, MDOT officials, and Ward 2 city councilmembers. That call revealed that the US-23 study's northern boundary had been revised before noise data was collected, a change documented in technical materials but never communicated to affected residents.

The Impact

Health, Wellbeing, and Property Values

The health and quality-of-life effects of chronic highway noise are well-documented. Residents along this corridor experience elevated noise exposure from two converging highway segments with no natural or constructed mitigation.

  • Sleep disruption: The World Health Organization identifies traffic noise as a leading environmental health risk in Western Europe and North America, citing chronic sleep disturbance as the primary pathway to cardiovascular harm.
  • Cognitive effects: Long-term exposure to ambient traffic noise above 55 dB(A) is associated with impaired memory and attention in both adults and children.
  • Property values: Residential properties within 300 meters of a major highway without noise barriers typically sell at a discount relative to equivalent properties further from the corridor. Barrier installation has been shown to partially recover that discount in comparable markets.
  • Equity: Communities already addressed by MDOT studies and approved barriers are, on average, higher-income than the northeast corridor neighborhoods. The absence of organized advocacy from Chapel Hill and neighboring communities reflects a community that was never informed or consulted, not a community without impact.
The Request

What We Are Asking

This is a resident-initiated advocacy effort asking the Michigan Legislature and MDOT to fund a sound barrier study and construction for the M-14 / US-23 gap corridor in northeast Ann Arbor.

Primary ask: A dedicated legislative appropriation for a noise study and sound barrier construction covering the gap corridor from the Pontiac Trail overpass east to the US-23 interchange, and south to Plymouth Road. This mirrors the structure of PA 121 of 2024, which funded the M-14 noise study to the west.

Secondary ask: That MDOT and the Legislature consider whether the M-14 study scope (currently Newport Road to North Maple Road) can be expanded to include the eastern segment of M-14 and the US-23 corridor north of Earhart Road.

Senator Sue Shink, who secured the PA 121 appropriation for the M-14 study, has cited the 1,000-signature Wines Elementary petition as the model that prompted MDOT to act. Demonstrated community support is the most direct path to legislative attention.
